01 · The Challenge

Food delivery has a choice problem.

Open any delivery app and you're handed hundreds of restaurants, thousands of dishes, and zero guidance. The result isn't freedom — it's fatigue. MoodBites starts from a different question: not what do you want, but how do you feel.

01

Decision fatigue

Infinite scroll across 100+ restaurants turns a hungry moment into ten minutes of second-guessing — and often a default reorder.

02

Sorted for the platform, not the person

Rankings optimize for popularity, ad spend, and delivery speed — never for the mood you're actually in tonight.

03

Cold and transactional

Every app looks the same: a grid of thumbnails and a search bar. Nothing about eating — a deeply emotional act — feels personal.

The opportunity

If the app opens with a feeling instead of a search bar, then AI can curate a handful of meals that fit the moment — turning an overwhelming catalog into a warm, single suggestion you can say yes to.

04 · Design Principles

Five rules behind every screen.

The constraints I kept pinned above the canvas.

01

Start with feeling, not filters

The first question is "how are you?" — never a search bar. Emotion is the entry point, not an afterthought.

02

Curate, don't catalog

Never more than eight picks per mood. The work of choosing belongs to the AI, not the person.

03

Always an escape hatch

"Surprise me" is one tap from anywhere. When deciding is the burden, we remove the decision.

04

One screen, one decision

Mood, then dish, then pay. Each surface asks for exactly one thing and previews what comes next.

05

Color carries the mood

Each feeling owns a hue. The interface re-tints around your choice so the app feels like it's listening.

06

Comfort over conversion

Warm serif voice, generous spacing, gentle nudges. We earn the next order by making this one feel good.

05 · Low-fidelity Wireframes

Structure before flavor.

Layout, hierarchy, and one-decision-per-screen — locked before any color or type entered the picture.

08 · Outcomes & Reflection

Fewer choices, faster yes.

A concept project measured against its own promise: would emotional curation actually feel faster and warmer than the scroll? Early prototype testing said yes.

≤3
taps to order
From open to checkout in three decisions
8
picks, max
A curated set per mood vs. 100+ listings
5
mood states
Each with its own hue and tone of voice
9/10
testers preferred it
Over their current delivery app, in a 10-person test

What worked

  • Leading with a feeling removed the "where do I even start" freeze testers described in current apps.
  • "Surprise me" became the most-loved feature — a pressure valve for the nights people couldn't decide.
  • Re-tinting the UI to the chosen mood made the product feel like it was responding to them.

What I'd explore next

  • Letting moods blend ("cozy but light") instead of forcing a single choice.
  • Learning a household's mood patterns over time to pre-warm the right palette.
  • Animating the mood icons on selection — a gentle motion cue the moment a feeling is chosen.
